Project EXTRA: VEX Smart Vehicle

Honors Principles of Engineering

February 9, 2023 - February 16, 2023

Project EXTRA, a new project introduced in the 2022-2023 school year, required students to design and construct a vehicle that could perform a variety of mystery tasks. The vehicles, after construction, would be given to a computer science class to program, where the vehicle’s effectiveness and ability to carry out tasks would be tested. The vehicle could only be constructed using VEX parts. Since the vehicle also needed to be programmable for the computer science students, the vehicle also required a battery, VEX V5 Brain, and various sensors such as a vision sensor and an optic sensor. With the expectations and boundaries of the project set, my group began strategizing various designs and mechanisms that could satisfy the project’s demands. We ultimately settled on creating a small tank drive due to its simplicity and ease to fit into the 8” x 8” x 8” maximum size restriction. The tank drive would feature a tower and crossbars for bracing, as well as for sensor mounting. Since the size restriction was very tight, we opted to position the motors in between the C-channels, leaving just enough room for the wire to be plugged in. The entire vehicle would then be braced using a variety of methods to ensure the vehicle was sturdy and did not break at the slightest inconvenience. As one of the lead builders in my group, I was tasked with a majority of the build-related tasks while other members created the How-To manual and worked on the project documentation. Upon creating the first iteration of the base, we realized that the vehicle would be in violation of the sizing restriction, meaning that a new approach had to be taken. Originally, the C-channels the tank drive was constructed from were oriented horizontally. As a result, the axles would contact the C-channels at two distinct points, increasing the stability of the wheels. However, since there was no possible way to push the outer C-channels in more, we were forced to flip the C-channels and use a single point of contact for each axle. With the vehicle within the sizing constraint, the rest of the vehicle could be created. I worked primarily on mounting the sensors and creating the vertical tower where the brain would be mounted, while my partners worked to add bracing wherever needed. Considering this project was to be completed within roughly a week, our team decided to divide the work evenly to maximize production and efficiency. Although we did several tasks independently, whenever an issue occurred, we immediately discussed it and worked the issue out swiftly. As evident, communication was a key part of our team’s success, as we were successful in communicating new ideas and questions, while also delegating tasks effectively. For example, while some members worked directly on the car, building new mechanisms, other members worked on documentation. To ensure each group member strengthened their skills, however, we rotated tasks to ensure that everyone contributed to each aspect of the project. Furthermore, responsibility played a large part in our team's success, as we consistently met deadlines and took care of our individual parts to earn the highest possible score on the project. The fundamental ideas that led to my group’s and my success are applicable to the real world, in both the engineering world and everyday life.

Compound Machines: The Stamper

Honors Principles of Engineering

September 16, 2022 - October 13, 2022

The Compound Machines Project, more formally known as P1.1.6 Compound Machines, was a project involving the application of various simple machines to accomplish a basic task. The compound machine had to be constructed using four unique simple machines, each of which was required to contribute the machine’s overall purpose in a significant manner. The compound machine would be built out of primarily VEX parts, however, other materials were also significant, including tape, string, and function-specific items. Given the requirements and boundaries, my group and I formed several ideas, ranging from fruit cutters to catapults, until we finally settled on one: a compound machine that stamps paper. According to our original design, the machine would be composed of a gear train, pulley, chain & sprocket, and a wheel. After reviewing the design further, however, it was determined that completing the machine in the allotted time would be extremely difficult, especially considering the amount of fine-tuning required for the design. To improve the original design, the chain & sprocket system was replaced with a first-class lever, which was significantly easier to create and was less dependent on luck. Under the new design, the input force would be applied to a wheel spinning an axle, with the axle connecting to the bottom of the pulley system. The pulley system is subsequently activated from the movement of the wheel and begins to pull a string. The string is tied to the pulley system on one end and the first-class lever on the other end. As the string is pulled, the first-class lever is tilted in such a way that raises the side attached directly to the pulley. On the other side of the lever, a string attaches the lever to a gear train. As the lever tilts, the gear train is pulled, ultimately placing the stamp onto the paper at the end of the gear train. Considering the project was relatively long in comparison to other projects and the sheer amount of unique functions that needed to be designed, tested, and prototyped, dividing the work was an absolute necessity to even complete the project at all. Several sections of the build were completed individually, while other parts were completed in pairs. Despite this, our group maintained stellar communication. This included asking questions frequently to ensure an understanding of how to build each mechanism, discussing any issues, and coming up with new ideas as the project continued. As always, communication and teamwork were principal to completing the project in a timely and efficient manner.

AP Computer Science Principles

October 11, 2021 - October 26, 2021

The Algorithms & Art project, more formally known as the PLTW 1.1.9 Project, was a dynamic project that combined Python algorithms and artistic talent. The primary objective of the project was to develop a program that designed a PSA regarding mental health, with the primary audience being students. Compared to projects in the past, this project contained several restrictions and specific criteria that had to be met. For example, the project had to include some form of animation, whether it be text-related or image-related. The project also had to follow all of the AP CSP Create Task rubric rows. To fulfill all of the requirements, my partner and I developed an interactive poster that reminded students to take care of their “personal garden”, a metaphor for a person’s mental and physical well-being. The program itself contained several different functions, each responsible for creating a unique part of the landscape portrayed in the poster. For example, the background, including the sky, flowers, and ground, were all contained within a single function. This function would then be stored within a list and called when the list was iterated through. 1.1.6 Creative Expression:

Computer Science Essentials

October 20, 2020 - November 15, 2020

The objective of this project was to create a functioning app that reflected a current major event. It didn't have to be worldwide, but it did have to have lots of attention towards it. The project did not have many boundaries except for this. My partner, who is the same partner I worked with on the Python project, and I decided to make an application related to the presidential election, as the presidental election was trending during this time. In this app, the user assumed the role of a presidential candidate who must make certain decisions to leverage the public in their favor. The game was split across six different scenes, with each scene revolving around a different event in the election process. Seeing as this project would take a significant amount of work over about a month long period, we chose to split up the code by designating each alternating scene to one person. For example, person one would do scenes 1, 3, and 5, while person two would do scenes 2, 4, and 6. For the first part of the project, we worked separately as we were simply developing each scene. We then began to combine them and worked on final changes together before submitting the final product.

In all parts of the project, there were several issues and problems that arose. By working together on these issues, we were able to successfully solve every problem. My partner and I’s success came primarily from our communication, as we were able to successfully communicate new ideas, questions, and issues, even though part of the project was done separately. Another key to our success was responsibility. We always met our deadlines, completed work outside of class, and went to office hours consistently to receive feedback.
